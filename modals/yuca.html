HOW TO MAKE YUCA FRITA

2014, USA

Animation (stop-motion), live action video

01min 39s.

Key Finding:

The combination of animation and music can help guide viewers through a process they may not have even been aware of before. The familiar structure of music combined with animation's suspension of disbelief highlights interesting opportunities for structuring information like a music video.

Context

This film was made possible with the support of Harvard VES, the guidance of Ruth Lingford, the music of J. Andrés Ballesteros, and the yuca from Bonanza Market.

Implications for Future Work:

  • Animation incentivizes its creators to break down a process into small, distinct steps.
  • Animation can help bridge language barriers.

Process

I started off by shopping for yuca in Lawrence, MA, at a bodega that has filled a local need by selling fresh + frozen produce in a food desert. I then practiced making yuca frita and, after feeling confident in my cooking, I made three batches of yuca to transition between on-screen: raw, boiled, and completely cooked. I then took these batches to the studio to make a frame-by-frame animation overnight in one fell swoop.


I had recently been having a lot of fun and surprisingly good results allowing some elements of spontaneity into my stop-motion food animation. So, I decided to go all in on spontaneity for this short. Knowing only that the yuca had to be peeled, chopped, boiled, fried, and served on-screen, I gathered the necessary props and made up the scene as I went along.


I originally thought this animation would make up one scene in [A Bodega], but found myself trying to squeeze it in where it didn't fit—I took it as a good sign that I had to cut out pieces of the project I loved individually. So, after finishing that film, I went back into this scene, fleshing it out with sound, music, + an introduction/ending.
